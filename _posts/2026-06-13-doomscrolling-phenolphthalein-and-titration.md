title: Doomscrolling, phenolphthalein, and titration
---

Dopamine rushes from short, mindless so-called “doomscrolling” fueled most of my days in the breaks between a life of academics. It was then I saw a video on *titration*. Despite having a somewhat mild interest in chemistry and having done modestly well in my chemistry classes by then, I hadn’t known about titration much less even heard the word before.

Turning the burette clamped on the iron stand open, a clear liquid flowed slowly into a less-than-half filled glass beaker, as I anticipately waited for some sort of chemical reaction to occur. Filled with a little misconception that every reaction had to be some sort of explosive, wild reaction (like pure sodium metal to water), I was entirely expecting for a myriad of things to happen: the beaker cracking, the liquid in it bubbling, among others.

Unbeknownst to my naive self, the solution would rapidly change into a quite light shade of pink, almost mistakenly clear yet undeniably shaded. I had just witnessed a titration. I would later return to class tomorrow then, realizing that the school had barely enough budget to allocate for proper lab materials, less so for chemicals.

Seeing as the dopamine had hit, the nature of Instagram reels eventually caused me to almost thoroughly forget I had even saw a titration, yet the word remained somewhere in my mind.

This lack of curiousity didn’t prompt me to even find out what made the solution in the Erlenmeyer flask pink; phenolphthalein, I would later discover.

Life happened and I eventually found myself at the doorstep of the analytical chemistry laboratory in my university. Having thankfully (*miraculously*) read the laboratory procedure just the night before, Activity 4 would be the first time I would perform a titration.

Having prepared solutions of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and hydrochloric acid (HCl), and jokes of “what-happens-if-i-spill-hydrochloric-acid-on-myself” aside, it was eventually time to perform the titration. 

*Showtime.*

How dramatic of a word to use when describing, somewhat a simple task: simply adding three drops of phenolphthalein indicator to the HCl and slowly opening the burette to the flask. I forget if I had, or my lab partner began the first test out of three, but eventually it was my hands, quite trembling in a mix of anticipation, excitement, and nervousness, clasping the stopcock in one hand (in the right way, as corrected many times by my instructor!) while slowly stirring the flask in the other.

That feeling of pure unbound joy with the first time just getting the right shade of pink; just after it blooms in color, yet before it is overrun with it? Unexplainable. I think I had – with shaky hands of inexperience – overran the first flask I titrated. That aside, I learned how inconvienent the so-called “proper handling technique” of burettes are.
	
If the stopcock is locked tightly, it takes a lot of force to turn it. Eventually, applying enough force, I over-rotated it and out comes a good milliliter or two. But I digress. Burette’s fault, not mine; and we’d later discover the other burette out of the two was better at miniscule adjustments. Presumably newer.

This was the first, of many titrations to come. While eventually the novelty wore off, titration was and is still one of the most fun things to do in the laboratory. Obviously, sometimes it might be difficult to get the endpoint just right, but there’s always back-titration (most of the time!). In some sort of way, titrations were a tick off my chemistry to-do-list. 

I suppose analytical chemistry is just a test of being obsessively detail-oriented, even down to the drops of solution. Oh, and most importantly,

*Don’t forget to add the phenolphthalein indicator!*
